Every time I mention Islamic fintech to a Western tech person, I watch them mentally file it under "niche markets" alongside vegan meal kits and left-handed scissors. That's the wrong frame.

Islamic finance isn't a niche. It's an alternative set of first principles about what money is for.

The prohibition on riba (interest) isn't a quirky constraint to work around. It's a claim about the relationship between money and time, between lenders and borrowers, about who bears risk. You can disagree with the claim — but it's a coherent position, not an arbitrary rule.

## the size of the "niche"

1.8 billion Muslims. A global Islamic finance market currently around $3.5 trillion. Projected to hit $6 trillion by 2030. The fastest-growing consumer segment in most Western cities.

This is not a niche.

## what fintech keeps missing

Most "Islamic fintech" is just conventional fintech with a fatwa bolted on. A rent-to-own mortgage that's economically identical to a conventional mortgage but structured differently to avoid the legal definition of interest. Permissible, perhaps. But not the point.

The interesting opportunity is building products that are *better* because of the constraint. When you can't charge interest, you have to think harder about risk-sharing. Mudarabah, musharakah — these aren't workarounds, they're partnership structures that align incentives differently.

<span class="arabic">وَأَحَلَّ اللَّهُ الْبَيْعَ وَحَرَّمَ الرِّبَا</span>

Allah has permitted trade and forbidden interest. The distinction matters: trade involves real economic activity. Interest doesn't have to.
